## Overview

Understanding how humpback whale populations connect across ocean basins is fundamental to assessing gene flow, population structure, and the resilience of recovering populations to ongoing threats. My research has contributed several landmark observations to this field, most recently the documentation of the longest recorded movement of an individual humpback whale.

## Key findings

**Australia–Brazil connectivity** (2026): Using photo-identification and genetic analysis, I co-led the documentation of a humpback whale that moved between the east Australian (E1) and Brazilian (B) breeding populations — the longest recorded inter-oceanic movement of any individual whale. This finding reveals unprecedented connectivity between what were previously considered separate breeding populations and has significant implications for international conservation management.

**Southern right whale range extension** (2024): Co-authored the first confirmed sighting of a southern right whale north of the equator, representing a remarkable range extension for this species.

## Implications for management

These findings demonstrate that international coordination is essential to the conservation of Southern Hemisphere humpback whale populations, and underscore the need for bilateral and multilateral agreements covering shared stocks.
